A Thousand-Year Soft-Shelled Turtle, a Ten-Thousand-Year Tortoise? How Much Longer Must We 'Take Things for Granted'?

The old saying, “a soft-shelled turtle for a thousand years, a tortoise for ten thousand,” is one almost everyone has heard. In Chinese culture, turtles and tortoises are seen as symbols of longevity, as if they are naturally born to outlive several human generations.

But here’s the question: Are there really any ‘soft-shelled turtles’ that have lived for a thousand years?

Many people, upon seeing a massive tortoise or turtle, will blurt out, “Wow, this one must be hundreds of years old!” Such claims might “sound reasonable,” but in reality, they don’t stand up to the slightest scrutiny.

1. Can Turtles Really Live for a Thousand Years? Let’s See What Science Says#

‚óè Chinese Pond Turtle#

Dark black in color, this is what we commonly call a “wugui” (turtle), and it’s the most common “symbol of longevity.” In reality, its lifespan is generally only 30-40 years, and even in excellent captive environments, it rarely exceeds 60 years.

‚óè Alligator Snapping Turtle#

With a fierce appearance and large size, it’s often mistaken for being “long-lived.” In fact, this turtle’s most prominent feature is its extremely fast growth rate. In the Americas, it’s a source for canned meat, and in farmed environments, it can grow by 10 jin per year. Their lifespan is typically 20-70 years, rarely exceeding a century.

‚óè Gal√°pagos tortoise#

This is indeed one of the most famous long-lived tortoise species on Earth. The living “Jonathan” is one example, having reached 193 years of age, making him the oldest known living terrestrial vertebrate. However, such individuals are extremely rare and represent the absolute limit of their lifespan.

‚óè Chinese Soft-shelled Turtle#

Adults typically have a shell length of 20-30 centimeters and weigh about 1-2 kilograms. Their lifespan in the wild is around 20-30 years, which can extend to about 40 years in captivity.

‚óè Yangtze Giant Soft-shelled Turtle ‚Äî also known as the “Yuan”#

In ancient legends, it was revered as a “divine turtle” and was the prototype for the creature that carried Tang Sanzang across the River of Flowing Sands in Journey to the West. It is enormous and is an extremely endangered freshwater soft-shelled turtle. The same genus includes the Euphrates soft-shelled turtle, which lives in Turkey and the Middle East. In 2019, a female Yangtze giant soft-shelled turtle at the Suzhou Zoo died at the age of approximately 90, which was a record-breaking age for the species globally. The male is also estimated to be around 90 years old. This is their maximum recorded lifespan.

2. My Personal Investigation: Stop Believing Everything You ‘Hear’#

The first time I heard the “thousand-year turtle” claim, and on television no less, I also found it quite fascinating. But reason told me: such claims require supporting evidence.

So, I started to do my own research instead of just stopping at the level of “I heard it on TV” or “a friend posted it on social media.” I followed this path for verification:

  • Consulting authoritative domestic and international science popularization platforms, such as National Geographic, ScienceDaily, etc.;
  • Reading multiple professional academic papers on the lifespan and growth rates of turtles and tortoises;
  • Browsing observation records and comparative data from specialized turtle and tortoise enthusiast communities;
  • Checking the breeding and lifespan records for turtles from major zoos and research institutions.

The results were surprisingly consistent:

  • ‚úÖ Fast growth ‚â† Old age

Modern science has long used methods like bone layer analysis, radioisotope dating, and long-term tracking studies to clearly confirm: No species of turtle has ever been verified to live for several hundred years, let alone a ’thousand years.’

üìå The verification order for any information should be: Authoritative scientific sources > Research findings formally published by scientists > Discussions in professional communities > Personal observation > Online rumors

3. The Bigger, the Older? Beware This ‘Intuitive Logic’ Trap#

Many people see a giant alligator snapping turtle or a Yangtze giant soft-shelled turtle and declare, “This one must have lived for hundreds of years.” This way of thinking is actually a classic cognitive bias.

The reason is simple: There are many species of turtles and tortoises, and their growth rates and adult sizes vary enormously.

For example:#

  • Chinese Soft-shelled Turtle: Adult shell length is typically 25 cm, with a weight of 2 kg.
  • Yangtze Giant Soft-shelled Turtle: Adult shell length can reach 50-60 cm, with a weight of several tens of kilograms.

But this doesn’t mean the Yangtze giant soft-shelled turtle “lives longer”‚Äîquite the opposite, it grows fast and matures early. In an ideal captive environment, it can reach its adult size in a dozen years or even less.

It’s like how among humans, there’s Yao Ming at 2.3 meters and others who are only 1.6 meters tall‚Äîbeing tall doesn’t mean you live longer.

Therefore, using size to judge age is extremely unreliable. Determining the age of a turtle or tortoise should be based on a combination of species characteristics, growth conditions, and scientific measurement methods.

4. Why Do We ‘Take Things for Granted’ So Easily?#

The “thousand-year turtle” saying is actually a reflection of our traditional way of thinking: valuing experience over verification, intuition over logic, and legend over empirical evidence.

In traditional Chinese culture:

  • We emphasize “intuitive understanding,” “harmony between humanity and nature,” and “the Tao follows nature”;
  • But we did not develop a systematic framework of mathematical logic;
  • We lacked the foundation of scientific methods based on hands-on experiments and repeated verification;
  • We are better at telling “stories” than at establishing “testable and falsifiable” hypotheses.

This has led us, when faced with many natural phenomena, to be accustomed to ’taking things for granted’ while lacking the ability for proactive verification and systematic thinking.

5. Science Isn’t Built on What ‘Sounds Reasonable’#

The true power of modern science comes from the combination of rationalism and empiricism:

  • Rationalism emphasizes mathematics, logic, and deduction;
  • But if detached from real-world observation, it becomes empty ‘idealism’;
  • Empiricism values observation, experimentation, and data accumulation;
  • But without a guiding theoretical framework, it can easily devolve into fragmented knowledge.

The core of science is: üî¨ Deduction + Induction, Logic + Empirical Evidence, Theory + Data.

But we often stop at “it sounds like it,” “our ancestors said so,” “a friend told me,” and even treat increasingly exaggerated claims as truth. Naturally, this makes it difficult to enter the real world of science.

Conclusion: Stop Making the Turtle the Scapegoat#

“A soft-shelled turtle for a thousand years, a tortoise for ten thousand” is, after all, just a cultural legend, not a biological fact.

Instead of immersing ourselves in myths passed down from our ancestors, it’s better to apply a bit of rational spirit and scientific method to face this complex and real world.

Science isn’t cold and heartless; it simply asks us to ask one more question:

üëâ Is it true? Is there evidence? Is it supported by authoritative data?

  • Stop taking things as true just because they ’look like it’;
  • Stop accepting things wholesale just because ’everyone says so’;
  • Stop letting ‘I’ve heard of it’ become ‘I believe it.’

Starting today, let’s say goodbye to ’taking things for granted’ and enter a world of logic, evidence, and critical thinking.
