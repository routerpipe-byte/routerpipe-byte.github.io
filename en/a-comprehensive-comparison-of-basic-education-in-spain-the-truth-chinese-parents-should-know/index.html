    üè´ Teaching Staff and Pedagogical Features#

    • Teacher absenteeism is relatively high in Spain. A doctor’s note for sick leave is legally binding and can be issued over the phone for paid sick leave.
    • Primary school generally follows a “generalist” system, where one teacher teaches multiple subjects like English, math, humanities, science, art, and physical education, limiting subject-specific expertise.
    • Subject-specific teaching only begins in middle school.
    • The saying “the gym teacher teaches math” is not a joke in Spain but a result of the primary school generalist system.
    • Exam scores are not ranked.

    üìò Curriculum and Difficulty#

    Many people believe that Chinese primary school math is much harder than in other countries, but this is a long-standing misconception.

    First, let’s look at age. Spanish children start school earlier. This is because Spain’s admission standard is based on the calendar year (starting from January 1st), unlike China, which requires children to be six years old by the start of the school year on September 1st. Therefore, on average, Chinese students in the same grade are about 9 months older than their Spanish counterparts, with a maximum possible age gap of 1 year and 9 months.

    Second, Spanish math textbooks introduce fractions from the first grade of primary school, whereas Chinese primary schools only begin teaching fractions in the third grade. In the final year of high school, A-Level math courses already cover calculus.

    Although the breadth and depth of Western math curricula far exceed those of China, their teaching structure has clear shortcomings: fewer classes, more holidays, and faster-paced lectures that rely more on students’ self-understanding and absorption. In contrast, Chinese math education is more solid in terms of class hours and systematic instruction, making it suitable for learners who need clear steps and continuous practice.

    Of course, international schools in China teach the same math content as in the West; the comparison above is only between public school curricula.

    üì¢ Additional Thoughts: Is Exam-Oriented Education Valuable?#

    Some criticize Chinese-style education as an “exam-oriented” system that produces “high scores but low ability” and is too hyper-competitive (“involution”).

    “Exam-oriented” is not inherently a negative term. The essence of being exam-oriented is to strive for good results in standardized tests like the Gaokao (National College Entrance Examination), which is precisely one of the fairest forms of competition in Chinese society today. It provides an opportunity for ordinary people, especially children from common or lower-class backgrounds, to rewrite their destinies and achieve upward social mobility.

    In the admission systems of many top Western universities, grades are often not the sole criterion. A student’s “background” is highly valued‚Äîsuch as family background, a golf championship title, or volunteer experience in Africa‚Äîwhich often becomes a bonus for children from wealthy families. This competition in “soft power” invisibly widens the gap at the starting line, creating another form of unfairness.

    Under China’s Gaokao system, anyone, as long as they are hardworking and excellent enough, can enter a top university based on their own merit. Excelling in the Gaokao is not achievable through rote memorization alone; it is built on a deep understanding and flexible application of knowledge.

    Starting from scratch in the early 1980s, China has rapidly accumulated undeniable world-second-ranking technological, military, and economic power, second only to the United States. Unlike the US, which relies on an immigrant-based talent structure‚Äîwhere over half of its Nobel laureates in science are immigrants, and business giants like Musk, Jobs, Brin, and Jensen Huang are also immigrants‚ÄîChina’s progress is entirely dependent on the engineering and technical talent cultivated by its own domestic education system.

    Today, Chinese individuals account for over half of the senior researchers in the field of AI; China has its own space station; it has become the “world’s factory.” All of this stems from the continuous stream of outstanding talent produced by its education system.

    Without competition, without “involution,” it’s impossible to cultivate a large-scale pool of engineering and technical talent, and the nation’s rapid development would not have been possible. The reason European countries can “lie flat” today is because of the centuries of accumulated advantages from their ancestors.

    Spain was once the world’s first “empire on which the sun never sets.” Spanish is still the second most spoken native language globally, with about 595 million speakers, second only to Mandarin Chinese. However, Spain itself has a population of only about 47 million. Its national decline is precisely because its society as a whole gradually relaxed, and its golden age passed.
