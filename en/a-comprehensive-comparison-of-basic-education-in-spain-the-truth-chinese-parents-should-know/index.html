    • Teacher absenteeism is relatively high in Spain. A doctor’s note for sick leave has legal standing and can be issued over the phone for paid sick leave.

    üìò Curriculum and Difficulty#

    Many people believe that primary school math in China is much harder than abroad, but this is a long-standing misconception.

    First, let’s look at age. Spanish children start school earlier. The admission standard in Spain is based on the calendar year of birth (starting from January 1st), unlike in China, where a child must be six years old by the start of the school year on September 1st. Therefore, on average, Chinese students in the same grade are about 9 months older than their Spanish counterparts, with a maximum possible age gap of 1 year and 9 months.

    Second, Spanish math textbooks introduce fractions from the first grade of primary school, whereas Chinese schools only begin teaching fractions in the third grade. The A-Level math curriculum in the final year of high school already includes calculus.

    Although Western math curricula often have greater breadth and depth than those in China, their teaching structure has clear shortcomings: fewer classes, more holidays, and faster-paced instruction that relies more on students’ self-study and absorption. In contrast, Chinese math education is more robust in terms of class hours and systematic explanation, making it suitable for learners who need clear steps and continuous practice.

    Of course, the math content taught in international schools in China is consistent with Western standards; the comparison above is only between public school curricula.

    üì¢ Additional Thought: Is Test-Oriented Education Valuable?#

    Some criticize Chinese-style education as a “test-oriented” system that produces “high scores but low ability” and is overly competitive (“involution”).

    “Test-oriented” is not inherently a negative term. Its essence is to achieve good results through standardized exams like the Gaokao, which is one of the fairest systems of competition in Chinese society today. It provides an opportunity for ordinary people, especially those from humble or lower-class backgrounds, to change their destiny and achieve upward social mobility.

    In the admissions systems of many top Western universities, grades are often not the sole criterion. A student’s “profile”‚Äîsuch as family background, a golf championship title, or volunteer experience in Africa‚Äîis highly valued, often becoming a bonus for children from wealthy families. This competition in “soft power” invisibly widens the gap at the starting line, creating another form of unfairness.

    Under China’s Gaokao system, however, anyone who is hardworking and talented enough can enter a top university based on merit. Excelling in the Gaokao is not achievable through rote memorization alone; it is built on a deep understanding and flexible application of knowledge.

    Starting from scratch in the early 1980s, China has rapidly accumulated undeniable technological, military, and economic power, second only to the United States. Unlike the US, which relies on an immigrant-based talent structure (over half of its Nobel laureates in science are immigrants, and business giants like Musk, Jobs, Brin, and Huang are also immigrants or children of immigrants), China’s progress has been driven entirely by engineering and technical talent cultivated by its domestic education system.

    Today, Chinese individuals account for over half of the senior researchers in the AI field. China has its own space station and has become the “world’s factory.” All of this stems from an education system that continuously produces a large volume of outstanding talent.

    Without competition, without “involution,” it would be impossible to cultivate a large-scale workforce of engineers and technicians, and the country would not have experienced such rapid development. The reason European countries can afford to “lie flat” today is because they are living off the accumulated advantages their ancestors built over centuries.

    Spain was once the world’s first “empire on which the sun never sets.” Spanish is still the second most spoken native language globally, with about 595 million speakers, second only to Mandarin Chinese. However, Spain itself has a population of only about 47 million. Its national decline is precisely because its society gradually became more relaxed, and its golden age ended.
