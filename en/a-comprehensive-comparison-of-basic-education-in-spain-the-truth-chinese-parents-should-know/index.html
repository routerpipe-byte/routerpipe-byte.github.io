ğŸ“˜ Curriculum and Difficulty#

Many people believe that primary school math in China is much more difficult than abroad, but this is a long-standing misconception.

First, let’s look at age. Spanish children start school younger. The enrollment standard in Spain is based on the calendar year of birth, starting from January 1st, unlike in China where students must be six years old by the start of the school year on September 1st. Therefore, Chinese students in the same grade are, on average, about 9 months older than their Spanish counterparts, with a maximum possible age gap of 1 year and 9 months.

Second, Spanish math textbooks introduce fractions from the first grade of primary school, whereas Chinese primary schools only begin to introduce the concept of fractions in the third grade. The A-Level math curriculum in the final year of high school already includes calculus.

Although Western math curricula surpass Chinese ones in breadth and depth, their teaching arrangements have clear shortcomings: fewer classes, more holidays, and faster-paced lectures that rely more on students’ self-understanding and absorption. In contrast, Chinese math education is more solid in terms of class scheduling and systematic explanation, making it suitable for learners who need clear steps and continuous practice.

Of course, the math content taught in international schools in China is consistent with that in the West; the comparison above was made using public school math curricula.

ğŸ“¢ Additional Thoughts: Is Exam-Oriented Education Valuable?#

Some criticize Chinese-style education as an “exam-oriented” system that produces “high scores but low ability” and is too “involuted” (hyper-competitive).

“Exam-oriented” is not inherently a pejorative term. The essence of this approach is to achieve good results in standardized tests like the Gaokao (National College Entrance Examination), which is precisely one of the fairest forms of competition in Chinese society today. It provides an opportunity for ordinary people, especially children from common or lower-class backgrounds, to change their destiny and achieve upward social mobility.

In the admission systems of many top Western universities, grades are often not the sole criterion. A student’s “profile” is highly valuedâ€”things like family background, being a golf tournament champion, or volunteering in Africa often become bonus points for children from wealthy families. This competition in “soft power” invisibly widens the gap at the starting line, creating another form of unfairness.

Under China’s Gaokao system, however, anyone who is hardworking and outstanding enough can enter a top university based on merit. Excelling in the Gaokao is not achievable through mere rote memorization; it is built on a deep understanding and flexible application of knowledge.

Starting from scratch in the early 1980s, China rapidly accumulated undeniable technological, military, and economic power, second only to the United States. Unlike the US, which relies on an immigrant-based talent structureâ€”where over half of its Nobel laureates in science are immigrants, and business magnates like Musk, Jobs, Brin, and Huang are also immigrantsâ€”China’s progress has been entirely dependent on the engineering and technical talent cultivated by its domestic education system.

Today, Chinese individuals account for over half of the senior researchers in the AI field; China has its own space station; and it has become the “world’s factory.” All of this stems from an education system that continuously produces a large volume of outstanding talent.

Without competition, without “involution,” it would be impossible to cultivate a large-scale pool of engineering and technical talent, and the country’s rapid development would not have occurred. The reason European countries can “lie flat” today is that they are relying on the centuries of accumulated advantages from their ancestors.

Spain was once the world’s first “empire on which the sun never sets.” Spanish is still the second most spoken native language globally, with about 595 million speakers, second only to Chinese. However, Spain itself has a population of only about 47 million. Its national decline is precisely because its society has gradually become more relaxed, and its golden age is over.
