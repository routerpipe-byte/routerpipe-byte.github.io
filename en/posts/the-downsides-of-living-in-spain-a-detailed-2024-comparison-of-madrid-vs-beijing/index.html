The Downsides of Living in Spain: A Detailed 2024 Comparison of Madrid vs. Beijing

February 4, 2026 · 6 min | Translations:
  • Zh
  • Es

Spain has historically been regarded as a country with a high quality of life in Europe, attracting a large number of immigrants and international students. However, when we conduct a side-by-side comparison with China, particularly its capital Beijing, we find that Spain has significant shortcomings in many core aspects of life. Based on the latest data from 2024, this article provides a systematic comparison between Madrid and Beijing across various dimensions, including consumer costs, education, healthcare, transportation, housing, home appliances, automobiles, and public safety, to deeply reveal the various inconveniences and cost pressures of living in Spain.#

1. Coca-Cola (500ml) Price Comparison#

  • Madrid: €1.50 ≈ ¥12.3
  • Beijing: ¥3

The price of a bottle of Coke in a Madrid supermarket is almost four times that in Beijing.

2. McDonald’s Big Mac Burger Price Comparison#

  • Madrid: €5.65 ≈ ¥43
  • Beijing: ¥25.5

Fast food prices in Spain are significantly higher than in China.

3. 95-Octane Gasoline Price Comparison#

  • Madrid: €1.57/L ≈ ¥12/L
  • Beijing: ¥7.9/L

Gasoline prices in Spain are over 50% higher than in Beijing.

4. Water Bill Comparison#

  • Madrid: €1.0–2.63/m³ (¥7.6–20)
  • Beijing: ¥7.9/m³ (First Tier)

For higher water consumption, water bills in Spain are noticeably higher than in Beijing.

5. Electricity Bill Comparison#

  • Madrid: €0.14/kWh ≈ ¥1.2
  • Beijing: ¥0.49 (Tier 1)

Electricity prices in Spain are approximately 2 to 3 times higher than in Beijing.

6. Natural Gas Price Comparison#

  • Madrid: €0.091/kWh ≈ ¥0.7
  • Beijing: ¥0.26/kWh

The cost of natural gas for residents in Madrid is nearly 3 times that in China.

7. Education Comparison (Public Schools)#

  • Daily Class Hours: Madrid approx. 4, Beijing 6-8
  • School Hours: Madrid 9:00–14:00, Beijing 8:00–16:00
  • Teacher Absenteeism Rate: As high as 20% in Spain, extremely low in Beijing
  • Vacation Duration: Spain has about one more month of vacation per year
  • IB Scores: Madrid average approx. 33, Beijing approx. 36

In terms of educational quality, academic intensity, and stability, Beijing has a greater advantage.

8. Hotel Price Comparison (Same Category)#

  • Four-Star: Madrid ¥2,172, Beijing ¥569
  • Three-Star: Madrid ¥1,960, Beijing ¥424

Hotel prices in Spain are three to four times higher than in Beijing.

9. Air Conditioner Installation Fee Comparison#

  • Madrid: Approx. €300 ≈ ¥2,500
  • Beijing: ¥80–200, some promotions offer free installation

Installation fees in Spain are as much as 10 to 20 times higher than in Beijing.

10. Air Conditioner Unit Price Comparison#

  • Madrid: Approx. €900 ≈ ¥6,800 (including installation)
  • Beijing: ¥2,500–3,200 (including installation)

The price of the same brand and model of air conditioner in Spain is about twice that in China.

11. High-Speed Rail Maximum Speed Comparison#

  • Spain: Approx. 200 km/h
  • China: Approx. 300 km/h

China’s high-speed rail technology and operational speed are world-leading.

12. High-Speed Rail Fare Comparison (Per Kilometer)#

  • Madrid: €0.15–0.30 ≈ ¥1.2–2.4
  • Beijing: ¥0.46–0.48

High-speed rail fares in Spain are 2 to 4 times higher than in China.

13. Public Hospital Appointment Efficiency Comparison#

  • Madrid: Average wait for a specialist: 95 days; for surgery: 115 days
  • Beijing: Regular appointments available within 1–7 days

The medical process in Spain is cumbersome, with extremely long waiting times.

14. Volkswagen Golf 1.4L Economy Car Price#

  • Madrid: €31,638 ≈ ¥243,000
  • Beijing: ¥152,263

The price in Spain is about 60% higher.

15. Two-Bedroom City Center Rent Comparison#

  • Madrid: €1,200–2,000 ≈ ¥9,200–15,300/month
  • Beijing: ¥4,000–8,000/month

Urban housing rent in Spain is significantly higher than in Beijing.

16. Safety Index Comparison (Numbeo Data)#

  • Concern about property crime index: Madrid 34.9, Beijing 28.6
  • Concern about violent crime index: Madrid 22.5, Beijing 19.6

Overall, public safety in Beijing is more stable, providing a stronger sense of security.

17. Metro and Bus Fare Comparison#

  • Madrid: Single ticket: €1.5–2.0 (¥11–15), Monthly pass: approx. ¥166
  • Beijing: Metro starting fare: ¥3, Bus starting fare: ¥2, with discounts available

Beijing’s public transportation system is efficient and inexpensive.

18. Kitchen Drain Unclogging Fee Comparison#

  • Madrid: Standard service: €150–500 (¥1,200–4,000), Emergency service up to €1,350 (¥10,800)
  • Beijing: Standard service: ¥80–200, Emergency service generally not exceeding ¥300

Home service prices in Spain are exorbitant and far higher than in China.

19. Mobile Communication Network Quality and Tariff Comparison#

  • Network Coverage and Signal Strength: According to data from institutions like Speedtest Intelligence and OpenSignal, Beijing outperforms Madrid in both the prevalence and stability of 5G and 4G networks. Beijing’s 5G coverage exceeds 95%, whereas Madrid still has signal dead zones in some urban areas and even indoors.
  • Price for a 30GB Data Plan:
    • Madrid: Average monthly fee is around €25–30 (¥200–240), with legacy carriers like Movistar and Vodafone being more expensive.
    • Beijing: The average monthly price for a 30GB data + calls plan from China Mobile/Unicom/Telecom is about ¥60–80.

Mobile communication costs in Spain are about 3 times higher than in China, and network coverage and signal stability are also relatively poor.

Conclusion#

From the comparison of the 19 specific life indicators above, it is clear that Spain (especially Madrid) lags behind Beijing in almost every aspect, including consumer costs, public service efficiency, educational quality, transportation convenience, and daily maintenance services. Although Spain boasts a beautiful natural environment and a slower pace of life, its high cost of living and inefficient allocation of public resources cannot be ignored.

Particularly noteworthy is the higher education admissions policy. Universities in Europe and America, including those in Spain, widely practice a “diversity admissions” system. Admission criteria are not solely based on academic scores but also heavily consider the applicant’s race and ethnicity. People of East Asian descent (Chinese), due to their generally excellent exam scores, are paradoxically classified as a “non-disadvantaged group” and face stricter scrutiny in the competition.

Data shows:

  • The average IB score for many international schools in Beijing is between 35–38.
  • Most schools in Madrid have an average score of only around 31–34.

Although students of Chinese descent achieve excellent scores, in internationally standardized tests like the IB and A-Levels, which use a globally uniform exam and grading system, the admission standards are identical. In this context, applying to universities from China is more advantageous than applying locally from Spain, as Chinese students generally have higher overall scores and are more competitive. It is important to note that even Chinese students who have obtained Spanish citizenship are still considered “East Asian” in the diversity admissions system. Therefore, they are held to the same standard as applicants from China, with scores ultimately being the key determining factor.

In summary, Chinese families considering moving to, studying in, or making long-term investments in Spain need to conduct a comprehensive and rational assessment of its high cost of living, slow-paced administrative system, and the latent inequalities in its education and employment policies.
