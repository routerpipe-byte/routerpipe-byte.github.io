In medieval Christian Europe, the rupture of language, the collapse of the educational system, and religious monopoly led to a systematic interruption of Greek mathematics, philosophy, and science.

Conversely, Greek knowledge was fully preserved, translated, and developed in the Byzantine and Islamic worlds, eventually returning to Europe through cross-civilizational transfer, igniting the Renaissance.

Many people subconsciously assume:

Since Europe is the successor to Ancient Greece and Rome, then mathematics, philosophy, and science should have been naturally passed down through Europe.

But the real history is quite the opposite‚Äî

In medieval Christian Europe, the rational knowledge of Ancient Greece experienced an almost systematic interruption; the one that truly preserved, translated, studied, and further developed it was, instead, the Islamic world.

This is not a value judgment, but a historical outcome formed by a series of ruptures in language ‚Üí institutions ‚Üí religion ‚Üí politics.

I. Ancient Greece: The Starting Point of Rational Civilization#

** (c. 6th‚Äì3rd Century BCE)**

Starting from the 6th century BCE, Ancient Greece achieved the first true rational leap in human history:

  • Mathematics was written into an axiomatic system: Elements (Euclid)
  • Philosophy was written as logical arguments (Plato, Aristotle)
  • Natural phenomena began to be explained without mythology

But this civilization had an extremely fragile prerequisite:

It was highly dependent on language, an educational system, and the transmission of manuscripts.

II. Plato and Aristotle#

Not “Ideas,” but a Complete System of Original Works#

Ancient Greek philosophy is not a “collection of views” pieced together by later generations, but a systematic body of knowledge composed of original works.

Plato#

(c. 427‚Äì347 BCE)

He conducted philosophical training in the form of dialogues:

  • The Republic: Justice, the state, the philosopher-king
  • Symposium: How love ascends from the sensual to the ideal
  • Phaedo: The immortality of the soul
  • Theaetetus: What is “knowledge”

Its core is the Theory of Forms: the rational world is superior to the sensory world.

Aristotle#

(384‚Äì322 BCE)

He was the first person in human history to systematically organize all branches of knowledge:

  • Logic: Organon
  • Ontology: Metaphysics
  • Natural Sciences: Physics, On the Soul
  • Biology: History of Animals
  • Political Ethics: Nicomachean Ethics, Politics

üìå He laid the foundation for logical reasoning, classification methods, and empirical observation.

III. A Decisive Prerequisite#

‚ÄîAll of This Was Written in Greek#

These works were all written in the highly abstract Greek language, relying on an academic educational system and a tradition of manuscript copying.

Once the language was broken and the institutions collapsed, knowledge would not “slowly decline” but would crash directly.

IV. Rome Inherited from Greece but Failed to Disseminate the Knowledge#

(From 146 BCE onwards)

After Rome conquered Greece, society became clearly stratified:

  • Upper elite: Greek language, Greek philosophy
  • Common people and the military: Latin

üìå The language of knowledge and the language of society diverged.

Rome inherited the achievements but failed to establish a universal system for education and knowledge transmission.

V. The Establishment of Christianity as the State Religion#

‚ÄîThe Transfer of Knowledge Legitimacy (380 CE)#

In 380 CE, the Roman Empire established Christianity as the state religion.

From then on:

  • Source of truth ‚Üí The Bible
  • Right of interpretation ‚Üí The Church
  • Classical philosophy and natural studies ‚Üí Pagan heritage

Reason did not disappear immediately, but it began to gradually lose institutional protection.

VI. After the Fall of the Western Roman Empire#

Did Europeans Really “Bathe Only Three Times in Their Lives”?#

In 476 CE, the Germanic mercenary leader Odoacer deposed the Western Roman Emperor Romulus Augustulus.

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, it wasn’t just philosophy and science that were fractured, but daily civilization itself.

One of the most intuitive and easily understood examples is bathing.

In the Roman era:

  • Public baths were widespread in cities
  • Hot and cold water systems, underfloor heating, and sewers were all in place
  • Bathing was both for hygiene and for socializing

In medieval Western Europe:

  • Cities declined, and public baths largely disappeared
  • The Church’s vigilance against “nudity and pleasure” increased
  • The idea that “bathing causes illness” became prevalent

This led to the popular saying we hear today:

“Medieval Europeans only bathed three times in their lives: at birth, marriage, and death.”

This is not a complete fabrication‚Äî“bathe as little as possible” and “not bathing is healthier” were indeed treated as a serious life philosophy in late medieval Western Europe.

When institutions and urban civilization collapse, humans may even voluntarily abandon the life technologies they have already mastered.

VII. The Divergence of Civilizations#

Greek Civilization Survived in the Eastern Roman Empire#

Meanwhile, in the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire:

  • The official and church language was still Greek
  • The original works of Plato and Aristotle were systematically preserved
  • Continuity was maintained in cities, education, and administration

But after the East-West Schism in 1054, knowledge was completely isolated by religious and political boundaries.

VIII. How the Islamic World Received Greek Civilization#

(8th‚Äì10th Centuries)

Completely contrary to today’s stereotypes, the Islamic world of the 8th‚Äì10th centuries was one of the most rational, prosperous, and cosmopolitan civilizations of its time.

After the establishment of the Abbasid Caliphate (750 CE):

  • In the 9th century, the “House of Wisdom” was established in Baghdad
  • Greek philosophy, mathematics, and medicine were systematically translated
  • Plato, Aristotle, and Euclid were fully translated and introduced

More importantly:

They not only translated, but also studied, collated, and developed the works.

When medieval Europeans first truly entered Byzantine and Islamic cities, what they saw was not “pagan wilderness,” but:

  • Running water systems and public baths
  • Hospitals, pharmacies, and university-like institutions
  • Prosperous urban life and international trade networks

üìå This is not praise fabricated by later generations, but a real shock that repeatedly appears in Crusader documents.

IX. Jerusalem: Different Attitudes Towards Non-believers#

This civilizational difference was particularly evident in Jerusalem.

In 1099, the Crusaders captured Jerusalem:

  • A large-scale massacre of the city
  • Civilians were indiscriminately slaughtered

Jerusalem under Muslim rule:

  • Non-believers were allowed to keep their faith
  • They could freely choose to stay or leave
  • They only needed to pay a poll tax to live there

This is not a moral judgment, but an institutional fact:

Only a society that allows multiple faiths to coexist has the space to preserve “pagan” knowledge.

X. Multiple Paths#

Greek Civilization’s “Detour Home”#

Greek knowledge did not suddenly “revive” in Europe one day; it underwent a long and tortuous civilizational transfer.

Three key paths:

  1. The Fourth Crusade (1204) The Crusaders did not go to Muslim territories but instead sacked Constantinople, and a large number of Greek manuscripts were brought back to Western Europe.
  2. The Arabic-to-Latin translation movement on the Iberian Peninsula European scholars proactively traveled to Muslim-ruled areas to study, re-engaging with classical works by Aristotle, Euclid, and others through Arabic translations.
  3. The westward migration of scholars after the fall of Constantinople in 1453 Byzantine scholars fled to Italy with original Greek texts, directly fueling the humanistic and academic revival of the early Renaissance.

In this path of return, the transmission sequence of Greek civilization is clear:

Greek ‚Üí Arabic ‚Üí Latin ‚Üí Europe

This was not a revival of antiquity, but a cross-civilizational, cross-linguistic, and cross-religious return of knowledge.

XI. A Decisive Rebuttal to the “Falsified Western History” Theory#

In recent years, a theory has become popular:

“Ancient Greek civilization was fabricated after the Renaissance.”

But this claim is untenable both logically and evidentially:

  • Original Greek works were preserved in Byzantium
  • They were independently translated by the rival civilization of the Arab world
  • And then translated back from Arabic into Latin

üìå This is cross-verification across multiple civilizations, languages, and religions.

If it were a fabrication, one would have to assume that:

  • Christian civilization
  • Islamic civilization
  • Byzantine civilization

collaborated in a forgery for centuries while being long-term adversaries‚Äîwhich is historically impossible.

XII. The Protestant Reformation#

Reason Finally Regains Legitimacy#

Even after knowledge returned to Europe, it was not “safe” at first.

It wasn’t until after the Protestant Reformation in the 16th century that Europe’s intellectual environment underwent a fundamental change.

With the unfolding of Martin Luther’s Reformation:

  • The absolute authority of the Pope was weakened
  • Individual reading and interpretation were recognized
  • Scholarship gradually broke free from direct theological censorship

Ancient Greek reason was finally no longer a “dangerous pagan heritage.” It once again became a knowledge system that was permitted, studied, and developed.

XIII. How Greek Civilization Ignited the Renaissance#

(15th‚Äì17th Centuries)

When this knowledge re-entered Europe, it directly stimulated the birth of a generation of geniuses.

  • Leonardo da Vinci: Anatomy, proportion, perspective
  • Galileo: Describing nature with mathematics, a direct inheritance of the Ancient Greek scientific spirit

üìå They did not appear out of thin air; they were standing on the shoulders of Ancient Greece once again.

Conclusion#

Civilizations Neither Disappear Nor Appear from Nothing#

The knowledge of Ancient Greece and Rome:

  • Was not fabricated
  • Nor did it die out naturally

It was merely obstructed by:

  • Linguistic rupture
  • Institutional collapse
  • Religious monopoly
  • Political isolation

When the conditions were restored, reason naturally returned.

History is not a matter of emotion, but a matter of evidence.
