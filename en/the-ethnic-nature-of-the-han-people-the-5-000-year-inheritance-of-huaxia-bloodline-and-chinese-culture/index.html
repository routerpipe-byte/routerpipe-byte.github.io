I. Mythological Beginnings: Yan, Huang, and Chiyou#

The Records of the Grand Historian and Discourses of the States record that:

  • The Yellow Emperor defeated the Yan Emperor at the Battle of Banquan, causing the Yan tribe to submit;
  • He then defeated Chiyou at the Battle of Zhuolu, ultimately achieving victory.

This is not merely a legend but a symbol of the integration of ancient tribes:

  • The Yellow Emperor’s group: An agricultural tribe from the Central Plains, with the surname Ji;
  • The Yan Emperor’s group: A tribe with the surname Jiang, related to the ancient Qiang people;
  • Chiyou’s group: The Jiuli (Nine Li) tribes, ancestors of the southern Miao-Man peoples.

Ultimately, the people of the Yan Emperor and Chiyou were incorporated into the Yellow Emperor’s tribe, forming the “Huaxia.”

VI. Genetic Drift: The High Probability of Minority Genes Disappearing#

Why has the Han O haplogroup persisted for thousands of years, while the genes of other ethnic groups have gradually dissipated?

The answer lies in genetic drift.

Genetic drift refers to:

  • Minority genes, having a very low proportion in a population, are susceptible to random fluctuations and have a high probability of disappearing completely after a few generations;
  • Majority genes are more stable and become more established with each passing generation.

This is like gamblers in a casino:

  • Large capital (majority genes), even with occasional losses, can still hold on;
  • Small capital (minority genes) can be wiped out and eliminated by just a few fluctuations.

Example from Human Evolution#

All humans today can be traced back to a single father, “Y-chromosomal Adam,” and a single mother, “Mitochondrial Eve.”

They were not the only people at the time, but:

  • Other bloodlines were gradually absorbed or disappeared through genetic drift;
  • Only their bloodlines continued, becoming the common ancestors of modern humans.

Meanwhile, “Homo sapiens’ cousins” like the Neanderthals and Denisovans eventually all went extinct.
