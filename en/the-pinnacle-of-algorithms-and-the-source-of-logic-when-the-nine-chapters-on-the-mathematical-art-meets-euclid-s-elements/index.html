    At the dawn of human civilization, two mathematical works appeared in almost the same era. Like two rivers flowing in different directions, they quietly shaped the completely different intellectual traditions of the East and West.

    One came from China, One from ancient Greece.

    They are, respectively:

    • China’s “Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art”
    • Ancient Greece’s “Euclid’s Elements”

    To this day, our understanding of science, technology, and even artificial intelligence still carries the DNA of these two books.

    I. Historical Context: A Philosopher’s Truth vs. an Administrator’s Handbook#

    In the history of Western science, the earliest and most influential mathematical work was Euclid’s Elements (the source of what later became known as “Euclidean geometry”). In the history of ancient Chinese science, the earliest, most widespread, and most influential mathematical classic was the Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art, compiled during the Han Dynasty.

    Comparing these two “most important mathematical classics of their respective civilizations” is not about determining a winner, but about understanding a more practical question:

    Why did ancient mathematics of a similarly high level ultimately contribute to modern natural science via two completely different paths?

    • Date of Composition: Circa 300 BC
    • Author: Euclid
    • Role: A “logical ladder” to the laws of the universe

    Euclid’s Elements was not concerned with harvests, taxes, or engineering costs. It was concerned with a more fundamental question:

    How should mathematics itself be defined?

    It was about establishing a “deducible world”: starting from the fewest possible premises to deduce as many necessary conclusions as possible. This is why many later Western disciplines regarded it as a “model for theoretical writing and scientific argumentation.”

    Some have even pointed out that the structure of Newton’s Principia Mathematica clearly borrows from the Euclidean system’s style of “definition‚Äîaxiom‚Äîproposition‚Äîproof.”

    2Ô∏è‚É£ Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art: Mathematics in Service of the Real World#

    • Date of Composition: Circa 2nd century BC ‚Äì 1st century AD (Western Han to Eastern Han)
    • Author: Compiled and supplemented by mathematicians over generations
    • Role: An “administrator’s handbook” for a unified empire

    It solved the most practical problems:

    • Measuring fields
    • Calculating taxes
    • Engineering construction (building dikes, granaries, digging canals)

    This was a book for officials and engineers. It did not pursue the “pure truth of the universe”; it pursued:

    Calculating problems correctly, quickly, and in a usable way.

    Because of this strong practicality, the study and application of the Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art continued in China for a long time, even extending into the Ming and Qing dynasties‚Äîit became a “standard algorithm library” for the ancient system of governance and engineering.

    II. Different Starting Points: Beginning with “Definitions” vs. Beginning with “Problems”#

    üìê Euclid’s Elements: Building a Universe from Nothing#

    Euclid’s opening does not calculate areas or solve equations. Instead, it first defines the world itself.

    Original Text:

    • Definition 1: A point is that which has no part.
    • Definition 2: A line is breadthless length.
    • Postulate 1: To draw a straight line from any point to any point.

    Here, what Euclid is doing is not calculation, but abstract modeling. The entire book is built on an extremely rigorous deductive chain:

    Definitions ‚Üí Axioms/Postulates ‚Üí Propositions ‚Üí Proofs

    This is a kind of mathematics that starts from “why is it necessarily true.”

    III. The Same Mathematics, Different Thought Processes#

    1Ô∏è‚É£ Pythagorean Theorem: Proving Necessity vs. Applying Effectiveness#

    • Euclid’s Elements In Proposition I.47, Euclid rigorously proves the logical necessity of the relationship between the three sides of a right-angled triangle through complex geometric constructions.
    • Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art It directly provides the formula:

      Square the gou and gu sides, add them, and take the square root; that is the hypotenuse. It then immediately applies it: to measure the depth of water, the height of a tree, or the dimensions for an engineering project.

    One proves “why it must be right,” while the other verifies “if it’s useful enough.”

    2Ô∏è‚É£ Engineering Problem: The “Official Calculator’s Showdown” over a Dam’s Volume#

    In ancient mathematics examinations (like the Mingsuan ke of the Tang Dynasty), there was a highly representative engineering problem:

    Prototype Problem (Shang Gong Chapter):

    Now there is a dam to be built. The bottom width is 2 zhang, the top width is 8 chi, the height is 4 chi, and the length is 12 zhang and 7 chi. Question: What is the volume?

    The Method (Algorithm):

    Add the top and bottom widths and halve the sum, multiply by the height, and then multiply by the length.

    This is the standard trapezoidal prism volume algorithm.

    If Euclid were to solve this: He would require you to prove the formula for the area of a trapezoid and derive it by breaking the trapezoid into multiple triangles. But a Chinese candidate would only need to: move the counting rods and execute the algorithm.

    This highly algorithm-oriented educational system gave ancient China an extremely powerful engineering execution capability‚Äî it could turn “governance” into something “calculable” and “engineering” into something “operable.”

    3Ô∏è‚É£ Positive and Negative Numbers: The Art of Red and Black#

    This is one of the most important contributions of Chinese mathematics to humanity.

    • Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art ¬∑ Fangcheng Chapter To handle the cancellation of coefficients, it introduced positive and negative numbers:

      Red counting rods represent positive, black counting rods represent negative. It also systematically provided the rules of addition and subtraction for “positive and negative numbers.”

    • In contrast Ancient Greek mathematics long considered “length cannot be negative,” and thus viewed negative numbers as absurd.

    Chinese mathematicians had an attitude more like that of an engineer:

    As long as the algorithm is logically self-consistent, it doesn’t need to be intuitively visible.

    V. The Key Divergence: Why Was “Nine Chapters” Long-Lived but Less Foundational than “Elements”?#

    The practicality of the Nine Chapters did indeed ensure that its study and application in China continued into the Ming and Qing dynasties: many later mathematicians often deepened and expanded upon the problems posed in the Nine Chapters.

    But it also had an unavoidable structural weakness:

    It provided almost no clear mathematical concepts, theorem systems, or systematic proofs.

    Euclid’s Elements was the exact opposite: it might not directly help build a dike, but it provided the “scaffolding for the edifice of science”:

    • How to define concepts
    • How to set axioms and postulates
    • How to prove propositions
    • How to build a self-consistent system

    This framework had a profound impact on later natural sciences‚Äî modern natural science not only needs to “know how to calculate” but, more importantly, “be able to build theories.”

    So the final result is: The Nine Chapters is more like an “algorithm library for governance and engineering,” while Elements is more like a “building code for scientific theory.”

    VI. How Does This Divergence Continue Today?#

    This difference between “algorithmic DNA” and “logical DNA” has become apparent again in the 21st century.

    • The West (Euclidean DNA) excels at going from 0 to 1: Neural network theory, large model frameworks, computational paradigm innovation Solving: Why the model is feasible
    • The East (Nine Chapters DNA) excels at going from 1 to 100: Algorithm compression, inference acceleration, engineering implementation, cost optimization Solving: How to perfect the model

    This isn’t about which is superior, but about different path choices.

    Conclusion#

    Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art is the wisdom of the earth, teaching us how to solve problems pragmatically. Euclid’s Elements is a contemplation under the starry sky, teaching us how to construct a rational world.

    To this day, we still stand at the intersection of these two traditions.

    True breakthroughs often come from:

    Using logic to test algorithms, and using algorithms to implement logic.
