    At the dawn of human civilization, two mathematical works emerged in almost the same era. Like two rivers flowing in different directions, they quietly shaped the completely different intellectual traditions of the East and West.

    One came from China, the other from ancient Greece.

    They are:

    • China’s The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art
    • Ancient Greece’s Euclid’s Elements (a translated title for part of Euclid’s Stoicheia)

    To this day, our understanding of science, technology, and even artificial intelligence still carries the DNA of these two books.

    I. Historical Context: The Truth of Philosophers vs. The Handbook of Administrators#

    In the history of Western science, the earliest and most influential mathematical work is Euclid’s Elements (the source of what later became known as “Euclidean geometry”). In the history of ancient Chinese science, the earliest, most widespread, and most influential mathematical classic is The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art, compiled during the Han Dynasty.

    Comparing these two “most important mathematical classics of their respective civilizations” is not about determining a winner, but about understanding a more practical question:

    Why did ancient mathematics of similarly high levels ultimately contribute to modern natural science via two completely different paths?

    Euclid’s Elements was not concerned with harvests, taxes, or engineering costs. It was concerned with a more fundamental question:

    How should mathematics itself be defined?

    It was building a “deducible world”: starting from the fewest possible premises to derive as many necessary conclusions as possible. This is why many later Western disciplines adopted it as a “model for theoretical writing and scientific argumentation.”

    Some have even pointed out that the structure of Newton’s Philosophi√¶ Naturalis Principia Mathematica clearly borrows from the Euclidean system’s “definition-axiom-proposition-proof” style.

    • Author: Compiled and supplemented by mathematicians over generations
    • Role: An “Administrative Handbook” for a unified empire

    It solved the most practical problems:

    • Land measurement
    • Tax calculation
    • Engineering works (building dikes, granaries, digging canals)

    This was a book written for officials and engineers. It did not pursue “the pure truth of the universe”; it pursued:

    Getting the problem solved correctly, quickly, and practically.

    Precisely because of this strong practicality, the study and application of The Nine Chapters continued in China for a long time, even extending into the Ming and Qing dynasties. It became, in effect, a “standard algorithm library” for ancient social governance and engineering systems.

    III. The Same Mathematics, Different Paths of Thought#

    1Ô∏è‚É£ The Pythagorean Theorem: Proving Inevitability vs. Applying Effectiveness#

    • Euclid’s Elements In Proposition I.47, Euclid uses complex geometric constructions to rigorously prove the logical necessity of the relationship between the three sides of a right-angled triangle.
    • The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art Directly provides the formula:

      Square the gou and gu (the two shorter sides), add them, and take the square root; this is the hypotenuse. It then immediately applies it: measuring the depth of water, the height of a tree, or the dimensions for an engineering project.

    One is proving “why it must be correct,” while the other is verifying “if it’s useful enough.”

    2Ô∏è‚É£ An Engineering Problem: A “Showdown” for Calculating Officials#

    In ancient mathematics examinations (like the Ming-suan ke of the Tang Dynasty), there was a highly representative engineering problem:

    Prototype Problem (from the “Shang Gong” chapter):

    Now there is a dike to be built. The lower width is 2 zhang, the upper width is 8 chi, the height is 4 chi, and the length is 12 zhang and 7 chi. Question: What is the volume?

    Method (Algorithm):

    Add the upper and lower widths and halve the sum, multiply by the height, and then multiply by the length.

    This is the standard algorithm for the volume of a trapezoidal prism.

    If Euclid were to solve this: He would require you to first prove the formula for the area of a trapezoid, likely by deconstructing it into triangles and then deriving the result. A Chinese candidate, however, would simply need to: move the counting rods and execute the algorithm.

    This highly algorithmic educational system gave ancient China an extremely powerful capacity for engineering execution‚Äîit turned “governance” into something “calculable” and “engineering” into something “operable.”

    3Ô∏è‚É£ Positive and Negative Numbers: The Art of Red and Black#

    This is one of Chinese mathematics’ most important contributions to humanity.

    • The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art ¬∑ “Fangcheng” (Equations) Chapter To handle the cancellation of coefficients, it introduced positive and negative numbers:

      Red counting rods represent positive; black counting rods represent negative. It also systematically provided the “rules of positive and negative” for addition and subtraction.

    • In contrast Ancient Greek mathematics long considered “length cannot be negative” and thus viewed negative numbers as absurd.

    The attitude of Chinese mathematicians was more like that of an engineer:

    As long as the algorithm is logically self-consistent, it doesn’t need to be intuitively visible.

    V. The Key Divergence: Why Was The Nine Chapters “Long-Lived” but Less “Foundational” than Elements?#

    The practicality of The Nine Chapters did indeed ensure its study and application in China continued into the Ming and Qing dynasties: Many later mathematicians often deepened and expanded upon the problems first posed in The Nine Chapters.

    But it also had an unavoidable structural shortcoming:

    It provided almost no clear mathematical concepts, theorem systems, or systematic proofs.

    In contrast, Euclid’s Elements was the opposite. It might not directly help build a dike, but it provided the “scaffolding for the edifice of science”:

    • How to define concepts
    • How to establish axioms and postulates
    • How to prove propositions
    • How to build a self-consistent system

    This framework had a profound impact on later natural sciences‚Äîmodern science requires not only the ability to “calculate” but, more importantly, the ability to “build theories.”

    So, the end result is: The Nine Chapters is more like an “algorithm library for governance and engineering,” Elements is more like a “building code for scientific theory.”

    VI. How Does This Divergence Continue Today?#

    This difference between “algorithmic DNA” and “logical DNA” has re-emerged in the 21st century.

    • The West (Euclidean DNA) excels at going from 0 to 1: Neural network theory, large model frameworks, innovations in computing paradigms It solves the question: Why is the model viable?
    • The East (Nine Chapters DNA) excels at going from 1 to 100: Algorithm compression, inference acceleration, engineering implementation, cost optimization It solves the question: How do we perfect the model’s implementation?

    This isn’t about which is superior, but about different choices of path.

    Conclusion#

    The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art is the wisdom of the earth, teaching us how to solve problems pragmatically. Euclid’s Elements is a contemplation under the stars, teaching us how to construct a rational world.

    Today, we still stand at the intersection of these two traditions.

    True breakthroughs often come from:

    Using logic to verify algorithms, And using algorithms to implement logic.
